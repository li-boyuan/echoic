The lighthouse keeper climbed the spiral staircase for the last time. After forty years of tending the light, his hands knew every groove in the iron railing, every creak in the wooden steps.

"They don't need us anymore," he muttered to himself, pausing to catch his breath at the halfway landing. The automated system had been installed last Tuesday. A blinking red eye that never tired, never forgot, never needed coffee at three in the morning.

He reached the top and stepped onto the gallery. The ocean stretched out before him, dark and restless. Somewhere out there, a fishing boat was heading home, its crew trusting the light to guide them through the rocks.

He flicked the switch one final time. The great lens began to turn, throwing its beam across the water like a searchlight sweeping for something lost. He watched it for a long moment, then turned and walked back down the stairs.

The light would keep turning without him. It always had, really. He had just been the one to make sure it did.
